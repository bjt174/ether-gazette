Headlines

Ohio Modernizes CRNA Practice Law, Ditches "Supervision" Standard

Ohio's House Bill 52 took effect June 8, replacing the state's decades-old requirement that CRNAs practice under physician "supervision" with a new collaboration model. CRNAs may now provide care at the request of a physician, podiatrist, or dentist, though a collaborating provider must still be physically present for critical portions of anesthesia care. The bill was sponsored by state Rep. Kellie Deeter, herself a practicing CRNA, and supporters say it will widen access to anesthesia care in rural Ohio.

AANA · Modernized CRNA Practice Law Secured for Ohio CRNAs

Multistate Coalition of Attorneys General Sues to Block California's CRNA Law

A coalition of attorneys general from roughly two dozen states, joined by the governors of Kentucky and Pennsylvania, filed suit in May challenging California's AB 876, which reaffirmed CRNAs' authority to deliver anesthesia care independently. The California Association of Nurse Anesthesiology frames the lawsuit as an attack on nursing education pathways, and CANA President Kristen Roman has publicly defended the law — a fight industry watchers expect to ripple well beyond California's borders.

CANA · CANA Responds to Multistate Coalition

ASA Issues 2026 Guideline Pushing Nerve Blocks Over Opioids

The American Society of Anesthesiologists released new practice guidelines strongly recommending fascial plane and peripheral nerve blocks for major cardiothoracic, abdominal, retroperitoneal, pelvic, and mastectomy surgery to reduce pain and opioid consumption in the first 24 postoperative hours. Pooled evidence showed roughly a 19 mg oral-morphine-equivalent reduction in opioid use, though the task force cautioned that the underlying trials were often small and methodologically uneven. Pediatric recommendations for open cardiac and thoracic cases were also strengthened.

NYSORA · New ASA Recommendations for Surgical Pain Relief

CMS's 2026 Anesthesia Pay Bump Largely Erased by Hidden Cuts, ASA Warns

CMS's CY2026 Medicare Physician Fee Schedule set the anesthesia conversion factor at $20.4976 — up just 0.88% despite a statutory 2.5% increase mandated by recent federal legislation. ASA says a new across-the-board "efficiency adjustment" and a roughly 7% cut to facility-based practice-expense payments wiped out most of the intended gain, while CMS also trimmed the anesthesia quality-measure set used for incentive payments.

ASA · CMS Finalizes Policies Undermining Anesthesia Payments

From the Literature

One Syringe, Two Drugs: Single-Syringe TIVA Put to the Test

Researchers at the University of Utah tested mixing propofol and remifentanil together in a single syringe — rather than running them on separate pumps — for total intravenous anesthesia during outpatient orthopedic surgery. Across 109 patients, the technique kept hypnotic depth in the target range (Patient State Index 25–50) for at least 70% of maintenance time in 77% of cases, with zero episodes of intraoperative awareness. Nearly half the patients needed a vasoactive drug to support blood pressure, and postoperative nausea/vomiting or transient delirium occurred in a small minority. For SRNAs, the appeal is a simpler pump setup with less tubing — the trade-off is that once mixed, the propofol-to-remifentanil ratio is fixed, so you lose the ability to titrate each drug independently and must stay alert for hemodynamic instability.

Bennion, N., Brower, T. S., Ballard, C. R., Steenblik, J., Lee, K. H., Egan, T. D., Swenson, J. D., Pace, N. L., & Johnson, K. B. (2025). Single-syringe total intravenous anesthesia with propofol and remifentanil: A prospective cohort study. Anesthesia & Analgesia, 141(6), 1230–1233. https://doi.org/10.1213/ANE.0000000000007581

Legislation & Scope Tracker

West Virginia Drops Physician-Supervision Requirement for CRNAs

Gov. Patrick Morrisey signed SB 810 on April 30, replacing the requirement that CRNAs practice "in the presence and under the supervision" of a physician, dentist, or podiatrist with a team-based "cooperation" model. The law took effect July 7, 2025. AANA now counts 44 state legislatures that have determined physician supervision is unnecessary under nursing law — significant in West Virginia, where roughly 75% of anesthesia providers are CRNAs, concentrated in rural hospitals.

AANA · West Virginia Recognizes Autonomy of CRNAs

Florida's HB 375 Would Let CRNAs Register as "Autonomous" APRNs

Filed for Florida's 2026 legislative session, HB 375 would allow CRNAs to register as autonomous advanced practice registered nurses under existing state law, practicing without an onsite physician-supervision protocol. As of a January 21, 2026 staff analysis, the bill had cleared its first House committee with a proposed effective date of July 1, 2026 — but final passage was not yet confirmed at publication time. Status should be verified before this date is treated as settled.

Florida House of Representatives · HB 375 Bill Analysis

Medicare Opt-Out Count Climbs to 25 States Plus Guam

The federal rule letting state governors waive Medicare's physician-supervision requirement for CRNAs — in place since 2001 — has now been exercised by 25 states and Guam, including recent additions Delaware (2023) and Massachusetts (2024). That's notably higher than the "19–20 states" figure still widely circulated online. Opt-out status is distinct from full practice authority under a state's own nurse practice act: a state can opt out of the federal Medicare rule while state law still imposes its own supervision requirements.

Knowledge Pearl

Local Anesthetic Systemic Toxicity (LAST) — the "lipid sink." When a local anesthetic crosses into the systemic circulation in toxic concentration (intravascular injection, overdose, or rapid absorption from a highly vascular block site), it can produce CNS signs first — perioral tingling, metallic taste, tinnitus, agitation, progressing to seizure — followed by cardiovascular collapse: conduction blockade, ventricular arrhythmias, and refractory cardiac arrest. Bupivacaine is the most cardiotoxic agent in common use because of its high lipid solubility and slow dissociation from cardiac sodium channels.

Per the ASRA Pain Medicine checklist (last formally updated 2020, still the current standard as of this issue), first-line treatment alongside airway management and seizure suppression is 20% intravenous lipid emulsion: a bolus of 1.5 mL/kg (ideal body weight) over 2–3 minutes for patients under 70 kg, followed by an infusion at 0.25 mL/kg/min, with a recommended upper limit of 12 mL/kg. The leading theory of action is the "lipid sink" — the infused lipid creates an expanded intravascular lipid phase that sequesters the lipophilic local anesthetic away from cardiac and neural tissue, restoring sodium-channel function and hemodynamic stability. Propofol is not an acceptable substitute for lipid emulsion — its lipid content is too low and its cardiodepressant effects make a bad situation worse.

This Week in Anesthesia History

June 25, 1800

Humphry Davy completed his introduction to Researches, Chemical and Philosophical, the work in which he documented self-experimentation with nitrous oxide and explicitly proposed that it "may probably be used with advantage during surgical operations." Davy was 21 years old, and his suggestion went almost entirely unheeded by surgeons of the day. It would take another 44 years — until Horace Wells's 1844 demonstration — before anyone seriously tested nitrous oxide as a surgical anesthetic, a reminder that the gap between a good idea and clinical adoption can span generations.

Board Question of the Week

During a general anesthetic with succinylcholine for induction, a patient develops tachycardia and, several minutes later, a steadily rising end-tidal CO2 despite an unchanged minute ventilation. Which of the following is the earliest and most reliable sign of malignant hyperthermia?

Correct answer: C — Rising end-tidal CO2. Malignant hyperthermia is a hypermetabolic crisis driven by uncontrolled calcium release from skeletal muscle sarcoplasmic reticulum (classically via a ryanodine receptor, RYR1, defect) in genetically susceptible patients exposed to a volatile agent or succinylcholine. That hypermetabolism drives CO2 production up before any other monitor changes, so a steadily climbing EtCO2 despite unchanged ventilation is the earliest and most sensitive bedside indicator — it typically appears before temperature even begins to rise.

Why the other choices are wrong: Masseter rigidity (A) can follow succinylcholine and may herald MH, but it is neither universal nor specific — mild jaw tightening occurs in some patients with no MH at all. Hyperthermia (B) is, despite the syndrome's name, a comparatively late finding — temperature often doesn't rise until well after hypercarbia and tachycardia are already underway. Tachycardia (D) is frequently the very first thing anesthesia providers notice, but it is nonspecific — light anesthesia, pain, hypovolemia, and dozens of other causes produce it just as readily, so it cannot be relied on to distinguish MH from routine intraoperative tachycardia.
