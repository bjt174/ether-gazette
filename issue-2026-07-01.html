📰Headlines

A New Protocol Cuts Unnecessary Eye Consults After Surgery

The Anesthesia Patient Safety Foundation has published a practical, anesthesiology-led protocol for managing postoperative corneal abrasions — the most common ocular injury of the perioperative period, typically caused by incomplete eyelid closure (lagophthalmos) under general anesthesia. Early data show the stepwise approach resolves symptoms without routine ophthalmology referral, sparing patients cost and delay while keeping serious injuries from being missed.

CRNA Ranks Projected to Grow 10 Percent by 2033

New workforce figures put the current U.S. CRNA population at roughly 67,700 — about 200 per 100,000 people — with federal projections calling for 10 percent growth through 2033, or about 5,200 new positions. The report frames the growth against persistent rural anesthesia-access gaps and an aging surgical population driving case volume upward.

Becker's ASC · The CRNA Workforce in 2026: 10 Things to Know

FDA Clears Single-Dose Propofol Vials as Manufacturers Chase a Chronic Shortage

Amneal Pharmaceuticals has won FDA approval for a single-dose propofol injectable emulsion, with production on a dedicated in-house line intended to insulate supply from the raw-material and contract-manufacturing disruptions that have kept propofol on shortage lists for years. It's a meaningful step, but as this week's Drug Shortage Watch below shows, propofol is not out of the woods yet.

Pharmacy Times · Propofol Injectable Emulsion Single-Dose Vials Receive FDA Approval

California's AB 876 Takes Effect, Formalizing CRNA Anesthesia Authority

As of January 1, 2026, AB 876 defines "anesthesia services" in California law and confirms that a patient-specific anesthesia order fully authorizes a CRNA's care, clarifying that medication selection under that order is not a "prescription." The law codifies long-standing practice rather than expanding it — CRNAs remain subject to existing supervision, credentialing, and facility bylaw requirements — but supporters say it closes ambiguity that had been used to challenge CRNA billing and hospital privileging.

📚From the Literature

ASA's New Pain Guideline Makes Fascial Plane Blocks the Default, Not the Exception

The ASA Task Force on Perioperative Pain Management now strongly recommends fascial plane blocks (e.g., erector spinae plane, transversus abdominis plane, PECS) to cut pain and opioid use in the first 24 postoperative hours for open cardiothoracic, abdominal, retroperitoneal, and pelvic surgery, plus mastectomy — with a conditional nod for minimally invasive cases and open hernia repair. Pediatric recommendations follow the same logic for open cardiac and thoracic surgery. The Task Force is candid about the ceiling on this evidence: many underlying trials are small, single-center, and inconsistently designed, so the "strong" label reflects a strong opioid-sparing signal more than definitive outcome data.

Joshi, G. P., Mariano, E. R., Elkassabany, N. M., et al. (American Society of Anesthesiologists Task Force on Perioperative Pain Management). Florida's HB 375 Clears Committee, Eyes a July 1 Effective Date

HB 375 would let CRNAs register as autonomous advanced practice registered nurses under Florida's existing APRN statute and practice without physician oversight — though individual hospitals could still impose their own protocol requirements. The bill has passed both the Health Professions & Programs and Health & Human Services committees; a companion bill, SB 462, is moving in the Senate. As drafted, HB 375 would take effect July 1, 2026, if signed — worth watching closely this week.

Florida Senate · HB 375 (2026) Bill History

The National Picture: Opt-Out Is Not the Same as Full Practice Authority

As of this year, 37 states plus D.C. have opted out of the federal Medicare physician-supervision requirement for CRNAs — but opt-out is a CMS billing waiver, not a scope-of-practice grant. Only 23 states plus D.C. currently give CRNAs true full, independent practice authority under state nurse practice acts; the rest still layer on collaborative-agreement or setting-based restrictions even after opting out. That gap between federal waiver and state law is exactly what bills like Florida's HB 375 and California's AB 876 are trying to close.

💡Knowledge Pearl

Sugammadex reverses rocuronium and vecuronium by encapsulation, not enzyme inhibition. Unlike neostigmine, which reverses neuromuscular blockade indirectly by inhibiting acetylcholinesterase and raising synaptic acetylcholine, sugammadex is a modified gamma-cyclodextrin that forms a tight 1:1 host-guest complex directly around the steroidal neuromuscular blocker molecule in the plasma. This encapsulation creates a concentration gradient that pulls free rocuronium or vecuronium off nicotinic receptors and out of the neuromuscular junction, back into plasma for renal clearance as an inactive complex.

The practical payoff: sugammadex can reverse even profound (deep) blockade reliably, works faster than neostigmine at comparable depths, and — because it doesn't act on acetylcholinesterase — carries none of neostigmine's muscarinic side effects (bradycardia, bronchospasm, secretions), so it doesn't require co-administration with an antimuscarinic like glycopyrrolate. It has no reversal activity against benzylisoquinolinium blockers (e.g., cisatracurium) or succinylcholine, since those aren't steroidal molecules it can encapsulate.

🕰️This Week in Anesthesia History
July 1940

In July 1940, the American Society of Anesthesiologists published the first issue of Anesthesiology, under founding editor-in-chief Henry S. Ruth, MD. It was a deliberate act of professionalization: anesthesia had only recently begun separating from surgery as its own discipline, and a dedicated, peer-reviewed journal gave the young specialty a place to build a real evidence base rather than passing technique down purely by apprenticeship. Eight and a half decades later, Anesthesiology remains the field's flagship journal — including, this month, the very 2026 practice guideline covered above.

🧠Board Question of the Week

A patient receiving a ropivacaine infusion for a continuous interscalene nerve block develops perioral numbness, tinnitus, and then a witnessed tonic-clonic seizure 20 minutes after catheter bolus dosing. Which of the following is the most appropriate next step in management, assuming the airway is secured and seizure activity is controlled?

Correct answer: C — Begin 20% lipid emulsion therapy

This presentation is classic local anesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST): early CNS signs (perioral numbness, tinnitus, metallic taste) progressing to seizure, which can precede cardiovascular collapse. Per ASRA's LAST checklist, once the airway/seizure is managed, first-line treatment is 20% lipid emulsion — typically a 1.5 mL/kg bolus over 2–3 minutes followed by a 0.25 mL/kg/min infusion — which acts as a "lipid sink," partitioning lipophilic local anesthetic away from cardiac and neural tissue.

A is wrong because ACLS-dose epinephrine (versus the reduced <1 mcg/kg boluses ASRA recommends in LAST) can worsen arrhythmias and impair lipid resuscitation. B is wrong and dangerous — more local anesthetic deepens toxicity. D treats benzodiazepine toxicity, not LAST, and doesn't address the underlying problem.
